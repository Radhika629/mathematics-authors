Georg Bernhard Riemann (1826–1866) was a German mathematician whose groundbreaking work laid the foundations for several major areas of mathematics, including differential geometry, analysis, and number theory.
His ideas profoundly influenced the development of modern physics, especially Einstein’s theory of general relativity.
Key Information about Riemann:
Born: September 17, 1826, in Breselenz, Kingdom of Hanover (now in Germany)
Died: July 20, 1866, in Selasca, Italy.
Fields: Mathematics – particularly in geometry, complex analysis, and number theory.
Major Contributions:
Riemannian Geometry: He introduced concepts of curved space and manifolds, generalizing Euclidean geometry.
This later became a key part of Einstein’s general theory of relativity.
Riemann Hypothesis: Proposed in 1859, this famous conjecture concerns the distribution of prime numbers and the zeros of the Riemann zeta function.
It remains one of the most important unsolved problems in mathematics.
Riemann Integral: He formalized the concept of integration, laying the groundwork for modern analysis.
Complex Analysis: Riemann developed new methods using topology and conformal mappings, including the Riemann Mapping Theorem and Riemann surfaces.
Legacy:
Riemann’s 1854 lecture, "On the Hypotheses which lie at the Foundations of Geometry," revolutionized the way mathematicians thought about space and geometry.
Though he died young (at age 39), Riemann's work opened entirely new fields of research in both mathematics and physics.
