Pythagoras of Samos (c. 570 BCE – c. 495 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher and mathematician, best known for the Pythagorean Theorem in geometry.
He founded a religious and philosophical school that had a lasting influence on mathematics, philosophy, and mysticism.
Key Information about Pythagoras:
Born: c. 570 BCE, on the island of Samos, Greece.
Died: c. 495 BCE, likely in Metapontum (in modern-day Italy).
Fields: Mathematics, Philosophy, Music Theory, Astronomy, Ethics.
Notable Ideas and Contributions:
Pythagorean Theorem: In a right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides (a² + b² = c²).
Belief that numbers are the essence of all things, influencing the development of mathematics and numerology.
Discovered mathematical ratios in musical harmony.
Advocated for the transmigration of souls (reincarnation).
Promoted a vegetarian and disciplined lifestyle as part of his philosophical teachings.
The Pythagorean School:
pythagoras founded a secretive philosophical-religious community in Croton (southern Italy), where members followed strict rules and studied mathematics, philosophy, and ethics.
Most of what we know about him comes from later sources, as Pythagoras himself left no written works,
and his teachings were often attributed to the group rather than the individual.
Pythagoras was both a historical figure and a semi-mythical one,
and many legends about his life make it difficult to distinguish fact from later invention.
