Archimedes of Syracuse (c. 287 BCE – c. 212 BCE) was a Greek mathematician,physicist, engineer, inventor, and astronomer.
He is widely regarded as one of the greatest minds in classical antiquity and one of the most influential scientists of all time.
Key Information about Archimedes:
Born: c. 287 BCE in Syracuse, a city-state on the island of Sicily
Died: c. 212 BCE during the Roman conquest of Syracuse
Fields: Mathematics, Physics, Engineering, Astronomy
Notable Contributions:
Discovery of the principle of buoyancy (known as Archimedes’ Principle)
Invention of ingenious war machines to defend Syracuse Development of mathematical concepts such as the method of exhaustion
(a precursor to integral calculus)
Invented the Archimedean screw for raising water
Proved formulas for volumes and surface areas of solids like spheres and cylinders Archimedes wrote many works,
most of which were mathematical treatises, including: On the Sphere and Cylinder
Measurement of a Circle On Floating Bodies The Sand Reckoner The Method of Mechanical Theorems
His works were originally written in Greek and were later translated into Latin and Arabic,
influencing medieval science and the Renaissance.
